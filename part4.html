Lady Baron Arrested...

In the year 2010, something happened that turned my life. One morning, there was an impromptu crackdown targeting our boss – the lady baron who supplied us with drugs. Her home was surrounded by heavily armed plain clothed officers. We usually visited the home every morning to pick bhang for our business and also take our own doses.

On that particular morning, I had woken up late. As I hurried to pick rolls, I got news that our boss had been arrested and that all the patrons and revelers who had been found in the compound were as well arrested.

An entire container containing drugs had been seized alongside with them. A total of 22 of my colleagues in the house of doom were in that group. They were taken to Bamburi Police Station and later to court at Shanzu. Many of them are still serving their sentences running up to 18 years! Only I and Paul escaped the trap. Word also had it that we were being sought, and so I had to quickly get out of that area…

I escaped to Maweni, where I had earlier met a fellow reveler who worked for certain Kalasinga. He was a mason and he offered to help me get some job at his boss’s yard. After 2 days of impatiently waiting, I was given work at the site construction yard, working as a watchman and also helping with woodworks at the lathe machine. We lived inside the yard, and so I felt secure there. We received our wages on Saturdays, and again, I spent all my money at drinking joints, irrigating my throat and pumping my head with bhang to come to terms with my tattered present, forget the past and worry not about any future – if there be any!

At sometimes, the Kalasinga had no work for me. So I got another means of earning little money. I became a hawker, selling glasses used as mboko – cups for serving mnazi. I became well known by the mamas who sold mnazi, across all the towns from Mombasa to Kilifi – and once more, the money was for financing illicit brew that I needed to keep myself high full time, I never cared even to buy clothes. I had only one shirt and one pair of trousers, and a pair of yeboyebo slippers….

The Prodigal Son's Return: A Second Chance at Life

In 2011, I made the journey back home from Mombasa. My family was in utter shock to see me. For so long, they had believed that I was dead. I had become emaciated, resembling a scarecrow more than a human being. I appeared weak and sickly, and many feared that I was afflicted with AIDS.

After two weeks of being back home, an old friend extended an offer to help me in charcoal burning. I needed something to occupy my time and generate some income. However, on the very night I accommodated him, I discovered he was attempting to bewitch me. He was uttering demonic incantations aimed at banishing me to Mombasa for a century! This bizarre episode, which was even broadcast by local radio stations, led to the entire community converging on our home the following morning. The culprits confessed that their intent had been to end my life entirely. God came to my rescue. The episode resulted in deep-seated hatred and enduring enmity between our family and some neighbors, which persists to this day. Despite this, I have long since forgiven them.

By 2005, my mother had become born again, finding solace and strength in her faith after years of suffering and loss. Traumatized by the deaths of both her husband and her son, she turned wholeheartedly to God. On the day of my return, she reached out to her pastor, Eliza, who came and prayed for me. For the first time, I embraced being born again.

Following the witchcraft episode, I began to contemplate returning to university. I prepared myself and traveled to Juja. However, when I presented my case to the Registrar for Academic Affairs, he initially refused to readmit me. It was only with the assistance of some individuals who explained my situation that he eventually granted me admission to complete my degree. Unfortunately, when I returned home seeking support, my family declined, asserting that I was still an addict and only sought money for drinking. No one was willing to assist me. Frustration and despair once again consumed me. I relapsed into smoking and drinking, this time in Mukuru Kwa Njenga, Nairobi.

In Kwa Njenga, I met several chang'aa revelers who offered me accommodation. My existence spiraled downward to that of a street dweller, living in Vietnam, a filthy slum where crime was rampant. I became a homeless, destitute man, going without food for days. My grandfather learned of my situation and even resorted to consulting a witchdoctor to "cure" me, but I returned to drinking even more after the nightmarish ritual.

I was now 33 years old, and there appeared to be no hope in my bewildering life. My family had given up on me. Yet, it was dawning on me that I needed to do something about the tatters of my life. For the second time, Pastor Eliza came and prayed for me. She offered guidance and made me start to believe that God loved me. Once again, I repented and accepted salvation. I made a firm decision to quit alcoholism, smoking, and chewing.

A Second Chance at Love and Redemption

In December 2013, I had an unexpected encounter with Elizabeth, the girlfriend I had parted ways with 11 years earlier. She had heard that I had resurfaced after over a decade in obscurity. She managed to contact my mother and insisted on speaking with me. After exchanging greetings, I openly admitted that I was not doing well. She encouraged me to remain steadfast. She generously sent me Ksh.1000 to travel to Nairobi in search of work. We scheduled a meeting at Uhuru Park for the following Sunday.

On that Sunday, I walked from Kwa Njenga to Uhuru Park. When Elizabeth spotted me, she could hardly believe her eyes. She had grown significantly in stature and was elegantly dressed, while I had grown feeble and weak. We settled under the shade of a tree and talked until 5:00 pm.

We met every Sunday in the weeks that followed. Elizabeth brought meals that would sustain me for nearly a week. I had yet to realize that God was at work, beginning the process of restoring what the devil had stolen from me. Today, Elizabeth is my wife, and we are blessed with two lovely daughters – Imani and Wema.

I entered into marriage while completely jobless. With no other options, I resorted to casual labor in construction (mjengo) in Emali for a daily wage of Ksh. 400. I remember vividly the day a heavy stone fell, shattering one of my toes. The excruciating pain reminded me of the warnings our teachers had given us – that life beyond the classroom was unforgiving. While at Emali, frustration piled up, and I once again succumbed to temptations.

A Persistent Pastor and My Struggle to Find Redemption

In mid-2014, I encountered Pastor Kaunda in Kaloleni, Nairobi. He would come every morning to our joint to buy mandazi. He never missed an opportunity to share the word of God, even if he found us in the midst of our vices. On multiple occasions, he prayed for us and invited us to his church.

Pastor Kaunda was not one to give up easily. One day, he invited me for a conversation at his house. He spoke to me about God's love and shared his own testimony of transformation. His story touched my heart. He almost wept when he learned that I was a university dropout wasting away. He emphasized that I could change.

Despite my doubts, I agreed to accept salvation. I began attending church, but my commitment wavered. After just two weeks, I fell back into my old habits. One day, while smoking at the railway line, I spotted the pastor watching me from a distance. Shocked, I slinked away.

In August 2014, I made the decision to return to Mombasa, settling in Mariakani. My wife remained in Emali, expecting our child. She prayed fervently, often fasting, beseeching God to intervene. However, my addiction persisted. Despite my job at CRBC Mariakani station, I continued to chew miraa and smoke bhang with increasing intensity. I was helplessly trapped in the clutches of addiction, losing every battle I fought to break free.
