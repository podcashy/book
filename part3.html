The Irrevocable Family Rift: Dad's Departure

During my early days of adjusting to high school and urban life, I received disheartening news. While I was still away at school, word reached me that my parents had engaged in a fierce and tumultuous battle, culminating in their separation. These reports, conveyed by my cousins in Nairobi, stirred up feelings of homesickness and deep concern for my siblings. My yearning to reunite with them grew insatiable, and as soon as we closed for the short April holidays, I made the journey back home.

Upon my return, my grandmother greeted me with overwhelming joy and had even set aside some money, a token of her love, painstakingly saved from her tireless labour. After spending a day with her, I ventured to visit my mother, who had sought refuge at her maternal home after suffering severe abuse and harm at the hands of my father. Her face lit up with gladness upon seeing me, and she rushed to embrace me, tears streaming down both our faces. The reunion was emotionally charged, and I cherished every moment with my siblings, who had joined her a week after her escape from Kilili town, where our family once resided.

On that fateful night when the irreparable rift occurred with my father, my mother fled for her life, leaving the young children at the mercy of kind-hearted neighbours. Dad, too, disappeared into the unknown, and from that moment onward, he never returned home, not to this day. His whereabouts remained shrouded in mystery, an enigma that persists even now. My mother persevered, working tirelessly in casual jobs to provide for the children as they lived at her maternal home. These memories remain etched in my mind, vivid and poignant, capable of evoking tears when they resurface in my recollections.

Divine Intervention: Sponsors from Germany Bring Hope

In the year 2000, during lunchtime, the entire school gathered in the dining hall, and I, a student in Form Two, was called out by the school captain during the announcements. The school director, Dr. Griffin, requested an immediate meeting with me in his office after lunch. An unshakable sense of apprehension washed over me as I stood outside the school's main office, pondering the purpose of this unexpected summons. Upon entering his office, Dr. Griffin handed me an address card bearing the names Mr. & Mrs. Groemling. With a warm smile, he informed me that I had been granted a private full sponsorship. He instructed me to draft a thank-you letter for the couple, which he would review before dispatching it to the sponsors.

The Groemlings, residing in Germany, corresponded with me frequently, assuring me of their unwavering commitment to fully finance my education. Their emails served as a constant reminder of their support, accompanied by pocket money disbursed at the start and end of each month. My time at Starehe became significantly more manageable and enjoyable, allowing me to assist my mother financially. During the ensuing holiday, I eagerly shared news of the sponsorship with her, and she expressed immense gratitude to God. In December 2000, the Groemlings visited Kenya, and for the first time, we met in person. They picked me up from school, and we convened for an extensive discussion at Fairview Hotel in Nairobi. They were keen to learn more about my family, and I candidly shared our entire story with them. God had truly come to our rescue, extending His grace to our family in a time of dire need. The Groemlings were profoundly moved by the challenges we faced, and they wholeheartedly offered their support. Every month, they sent additional funds, beyond my pocket money and school fees, to provide for my mother and siblings. It was an enormous relief for our family, particularly my hardworking mother.

In 2001, the Groemlings revisited Kenya, and this time, we journeyed to my hometown together. It was a momentous occasion for me, my family, and even the entire village, as they witnessed me returning home in the company of "wazungus" (foreigners). We also paid a visit to my former primary school head teacher, capturing the moment with photographs. The Groemlings had the opportunity to validate the information I had shared about my family. Later that day, we convened at Rangers Hotel in Nairobi National Park. During our discussion, they reaffirmed their commitment to extend additional assistance to our impoverished family. Every academic term, they received my performance report from Starehe, and they were pleased with my progress. I had always dreamt of becoming a doctor, a dream they wholeheartedly encouraged. To me, they were more than sponsors; they became my parents, and I was their cherished son.

A Shift in Academic Path and a Transformational Family Move

In 2002, I took the KCSE (Kenya Certificate of Secondary Education) exams and achieved an outstanding aggregate grade of A- (minus). With 46 cluster points, I fell just one point short of the entry requirements for a medicine degree, which was a deeply cherished dream of mine. This twist of fate led me to choose a different academic path, opting for a degree in computer science. I shared this decision with my sponsors, the Groemlings, and they wholeheartedly supported my choice. Soon after completing my KCSE exams, the Groemlings made plans to relocate my family to a new area. They had already discussed this with Vincent and entrusted him with the task of finding suitable land. Vincent identified a promising parcel in Kibwezi, and the Groemlings financed the acquisition of this 10-acre plot. They also generously funded the construction of modern houses and water tanks. In 2003, while awaiting my university admission, I assumed the role of overseeing the construction project. It took an entire year to complete the ambitious undertaking.

In December 2003, my family experienced a transformative moment as we bid farewell to our traditional mud-walled, grass-thatched huts, notorious for their bed-bug infestations. We relocated to modern, well-furnished houses, replete with the luxury of comfortable beds and mattresses, sturdy iron-sheet roofs, and the convenience of electric lighting at the flick of a switch. For the first time in their lives, my mother and siblings savored the comforts of modern living. It was an extraordinary relief and a testament to the miraculous intervention of God in our lives.

Following the successful completion of the project and our resettlement, the Groemlings visited Kenya once more. We met in Mombasa and enjoyed a meal at Leopards Beach Hotel in Ukunda. The following day, we traveled to Kibwezi, where they personally verified the success of the project and acknowledged that I had exceeded their expectations in supervising the construction. Subsequently, we convened for another meeting at Fairview Hotel in Nairobi, where we discussed my upcoming university journey and further support for my family in our newfound home in Kibwezi.

A Downward Spiral: The Devil's Deception

The devil, they say, is a master of deception, and he specializes in stealing the good things in life. He's a cunning thief, always targeting what is valuable, precious, and important. The thief doesn't break in to steal worthless items; no, he goes after what holds true worth. His strategy is to find a way into your life, seize control, hijack your destiny, and leave you in ruins.

In 2004, I embarked on my journey at JKUAT (Jomo Kenyatta University of Agriculture and Technology) to pursue a degree in computer science. Financially, I wanted for nothing; my sponsors covered my tuition fees and provided me with pocket money. I was assigned to room 404 in the university's halls of residence, sharing the cubicle with three other students. However, my roommates, hailing from different corners of the country, had a penchant for alcohol. On weekends, they would purchase wines and spirits and indulge in raucous partying right within our cubicle. They often bought soda for me, knowing I had never partaken in drinking. I would watch in amazement as they danced with their girlfriends, openly smoked, and engaged in reckless behavior. Sometimes, the embarrassment would drive me to the library, where I sought solace to avoid their company.

The devil, however, is relentless in his deceptions. His aim is to ensnare, to lure good people into his web of temptation. He employs seduction, ridicule, and mockery to coax you into trying things you once vehemently refused. I too succumbed to the pressure. One day, I took a sip of Ritchot diluted with soda, and even that small amount left me feeling dizzy. Curiosity got the better of me, and I tried it again on another occasion, sipping a little more. Gradually, I became accustomed to it, progressing to the point where I could handle a quarter bottle on my own. Over time, I began buying full bottles of wine from the bar and consuming them from the comfort of my bed in the cubicle. I even started smoking cigars, courtesy of a friend named Karanja. At first, I choked, but eventually, I adapted to smoking as well. I began purchasing cigarettes and indulging in smoking whenever I pleased, even during my free time when I wasn't in class.

This marked the beginning of a perilous descent into uncontrolled alcoholism, drug and substance abuse, which ultimately led to my painful downfall. I lost my sponsors, dropped out of university, and endured 12 long years of rejection and struggle. It seemed as if everyone had turned their backs on me, expecting my imminent demise. However, in the midst of this darkness, God intervened and rescued me, renewing my hope and offering me a chance at redemption.

Beware of Your Companions - A Costly Choice of Friends

As the first academic year drew to a close, I found myself deeply entrenched in the world of smoking and drinking. I had become addicted to nicotine, unable to go without a cigarette. I had ample financial resources to sustain these destructive habits, thanks to my sponsors who continued to provide full support. I had also become part of a circle of dubious friends who shared my penchant for heavy smoking and drinking. Some of my former high school classmates, who had joined me at the university, observed this troubling transformation and voiced their concerns. They couldn't understand why I had succumbed to these habits, which were entirely foreign to the person they once knew me to be. They tried to approach me about it, but I actively avoided them. I stopped visiting them in their cubicles and distanced myself from them simply because they cared enough to be concerned about my descent into alcoholism. Instead, I chose to socialize with those who shared my vices, heading to bars in the nearby Gachororo village with Karanja and his associates.

Upon completing our exams, the long vacation beckoned. I returned home for approximately a month. Despite being home, I continued to indulge in drinking and smoking, but I made sure to conceal these activities from my mother. I would venture to distant towns, spending nights out with friends, engaging in drinking, chewing, and smoking. The devil had firmly ensnared me, hijacking my life and confining me to his treacherous domain. He blinded me to the perilous precipice looming ahead on the path I had chosen. He deafened my ears, rendering me impervious to the warnings issued by true friends who cared about my well-being. That's precisely the modus operandi of the devil when he seizes control of your life. He recognized that I had come close to achieving victory, and he devised a cunning plan to drag me down and plunge me into the abyss of self-destruction.

The Fall from Grace: Into the Abyss of Addiction and Despair

I plummeted from grace to a life of destitution. I earned a notorious reputation as a heavy drinker and miraa enthusiast, hailed as a hero when it came to buying beer and khat. I was surrounded by friends who relished in these habits, and they were drawn to me because of my financial generosity. I felt that I was adored by everyone, and I didn't discriminate when it came to friends – whoever crossed my path received whatever they desired. I even had a girlfriend named Elizabeth, who lived nearby in the same village. She grew increasingly alarmed at my unbridled alcoholism, but whenever she raised concerns, I dismissed them, sometimes leading to heated arguments. When the devil ensnares you, he ensures that you're shielded from those who could rescue you from his grasp. My girlfriend grew frustrated and eventually left for Nairobi in search of employment. We parted ways, and I, too, became disheartened. I decided to travel to Mombasa in pursuit of work, as an uncle had promised to help me secure a job at the Kenya Ports Authority (KPA).

Regrettably, I didn't obtain the promised job in Mombasa. Instead, my uncle wanted me to assist him in his grocery store. He remained unaware of my drinking and smoking habits, as I kept them well hidden. I would often disappear to Moroto slums in Tudor, where I had already formed friendships at the mnazi brew dens. I indulged in mnazi palm wine and began experimenting with smoking marijuana (bhang). When I sensed that my uncle had become aware of my drinking exploits, I decided never to set foot in his house again. He conveyed the news to my mother that I had fallen in with a bad crowd. One of my new acquaintances from Moroto offered to let me stay in his house at Maweni, Kongowea, and I promptly relocated. I took care to ensure that I would never encounter my uncle again, even changing my phone number. For the next seven years, I had no communication with my family.

When the second academic year at university commenced, I chose not to return. My family searched for me in vain; my sponsors contacted Vincent, who informed them that my whereabouts were unknown. The university sent a letter of notification to my former primary school, and the head teacher, who had facilitated my admission to Starehe, delivered it to my mother. The letter confirmed that I was no longer enrolled in the university, as I had not reported for three months into the academic year. Concerns about my disappearance grew, and reports reached my sponsors that I had left school. They sent me an email, which I read while peddling bhang in Mtwapa, Mombasa. However, I never responded to their inquiries, and they eventually grew frustrated. They informed me that they were terminating their sponsorship and financial support, citing my dishonesty about being in school while I had actually dropped out and succumbed to alcoholism. My frustration mounted, driving me to drink even more. I consumed any alcoholic beverage I could find, smoked bhang, and chewed miraa in a futile attempt to escape the harsh reality. Desperation and necessity forced me to seek any form of manual labor, but I found none. I went days without food, sleeping in corridors and on the streets. As soon as my friends realized I had no money, they abandoned me. I could no longer afford to buy beer, and the bitter truth began to sink in – I had to find a way to earn a living.

One day, while wandering around Kongowea market, I encountered Paul, a fellow reveler and drunkard. I confided in him about my struggles, concealing my true academic status and concocting lies to elicit sympathy. I told him that I was eager to find work of any kind to support myself. Paul was a peddler of bhang, and I had known him for about three months. He invited me to stay with him in Vikwatani and offered me shelter for some time. We slept on the floor, using a few torn cardboard boxes as makeshift mattresses. He introduced me to peddling and I quickly adapted, becoming deeply entrenched in the cheap, illegal trade, selling rolls of bhang to revelers in Kisauni and Mtopanga. Paul also introduced me to his bhang supplier, a female drug baron who dealt in cocaine and heroin. She had built an empire from her illicit activities, and I became a trafficker of drugs. This allowed me to earn a modest income to sustain my drinking habits and procure readily available drugs. I lived with Paul for three years, during which I remained trapped in addiction. I had comfortably settled into a downward spiral, convinced that I had already fallen and resigned to a bleak future. We consumed mnazi palm wine and chang'aa voraciously, and we smoked bhang. We had a large circle of daily patrons and revelers. Occasionally, we faced arrest, but the influential woman who ran the operation ensured that we were never locked up in the squalid cells of Bamburi Police Station. She was immensely wealthy and held sway over senior officers who shielded her illicit enterprise.

For seven long years, I called the coast region my home. During this time, I worked as a peddler and was heavily addicted to dangerous substances. I peddled drugs in Kisauni, Mtopanga, Mtwapa, Kilifi, Ukunda, Malindi, Likoni, and Changamwe, among other places. When there were crackdowns and we couldn't sell drugs, I resorted to degrading, menial jobs I never imagined I'd undertake in my life. I distinctly remember several occasions when we emptied pit latrines and sewage systems throughout the night, with all the money earned going towards drinking. Paul, like me, never returned to his family in Matuu. It seemed he, too, was on the run, escaping from himself. Msanii, another peddler originally from western Kenya, was arrested and sentenced to five years in prison, where he converted to Islam and remains on the coast. Moni was arrested and sentenced to eight years but has since completed his term and reformed, working as a mason. Settler became an artist, producing a number of tracks and finding success in his art. I recently crossed paths with Brother Paul, who has transformed into a pastor, preaching messages of hope and restoration following his narrow escape from being lynched by an angry mob...
