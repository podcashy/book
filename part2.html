Introduction

In the realm of human endeavour, work is our constant companion. To lighten the burden and enhance our efficiency, we turn to tools. In this modern age of technological marvels, scientific progress has gifted us with an array of ingenious instruments, simplifying a multitude of tasks. However, these tools are not thrust into action haphazardly; they undergo rigorous scrutiny before they earn their place in our work. Take, for instance, the humble act of felling a tree; a sharp axe is essential, ensuring minimal effort is required. Thus, before embarking on this arduous task, the axe must undergo a meticulous sharpening process. It must be meticulously ground against the grinding stone until it achieves the optimal sharpness, guaranteeing superior results. Often, one may even conduct a preliminary test, confirming the axe's suitability for the mission at hand.

In the divine theatre of creation, God too employs tools to accomplish His grand designs upon humanity. His chosen instruments are individuals, carefully selected for a specific purpose. When God singles you out and commissions you for His divine mission, a profound transformation awaits. Yet, the journey of preparation is anything but facile, especially when the chosen vessel is obstinate, rebellious, or resistant to their assignment. And once God has designated you as His instrument, escape is futile. History attests to this immutable truth; consider Saul's conversion to Paul or Jonah's reluctant voyage to Nineveh, where he ultimately fulfilled God's mandate. No matter how ardently one may attempt to evade divine calling, God possesses an infallible mechanism for humbling those who refuse to heed His summons.

I, too, found myself ensnared in the web of rebellion and evasion of God's divine call. For a protracted period, I harboured arrogance and disobedience within my heart. The malevolent forces of darkness held me captive for a daunting twelve years, during which I wandered in the wilderness. Abandoned by family, friends, and even kin, I was left isolated, as they speculated about my demise. Little did I realise that God had a message for me, one that necessitated my isolation—a time of preparation when I had no choice but to wholeheartedly rely on Him.

To convey the profound message contained within this book, I've meticulously recounted the pivotal moments that transpired in my life. My testimony is extensive, an ode to the glory of God, but it would be impossible to encapsulate it all within these pages. What follows is a condensed narrative of my twelve-year odyssey—a journey that culminated in the profound message entrusted to me by God...

A Modest Beginning: Family, Love, and Change

My journey began on the 10th of May, 1983, into the embrace of a humble family. My mother, Anjelica Mumbua, and my father, John Musembi, were the pillars of our household. I am the eldest of six siblings, with three brothers and two sisters. My mother dedicated her life to being a homemaker, while my father, until the fateful year of 1999, pursued a career as a primary school teacher. However, it was a career marred by a relentless battle with alcoholism, a demon that ultimately led to his departure from our family, a decision cloaked in mystery to this day.

I vividly recall the turbulent times when my father's recurring bouts of inebriation led to his frequent suspension by his employer, the Teachers Service Commission (TSC). These suspensions culminated in his eventual dismissal from work. The weight of frustrations, desperation, poverty, and the discord stemming from unfulfilled responsibilities seemed to have driven him to flee from his obligations.

The early years of my life, set amidst the backdrop of the 1990s, were graced with the boundless love of my parents. They showered me with the affection reserved for a firstborn son, and I treasure countless memories of being the recipient of their adoration. My father's profession even saw him teaching at the very school I attended for my lower primary education, Yindundu Primary School, nestled in the Kibwezi sub-county of Makueni County.

Our family's migration to Yindundu occurred when my father received a transfer from Ndolo Secondary School, located in the upper reaches of Makueni County, Kilome sub-county, atop the picturesque Kilungu hills. At Ndolo, he had served as a secondary school teacher before the government reassigned him to a primary school, where he assumed the role of a senior teacher.

A Foundation of Faith and Early Achievements

In my formative years, my parents cultivated a strong Christian upbringing for me. Each Sunday morning, I joined my friends on the path to Yindundu Catholic Church, a place where my youthful heart found joy in singing and dancing. I embraced my musical talents with zeal, even participating in spirited competitions and securing a coveted spot in the Sunday school choir. Academically, I excelled in my studies, earning the affection of my father, who held me in high regard.

Our life was firmly rooted in Yindundu until 1993, a time when teachers in remote regions like ours undertook arduous journeys to the Machakos District Education Officer's office to collect their salaries. My father embarked on these taxing trips, often spanning two days, only returning on the third day. Those were different times; the scarcity of vehicles made transportation a formidable challenge. On one fateful month-end, he bid our family farewell, as was his routine, setting out to collect his wages. We anticipated his return with excitement, envisioning the customary presents and delights he would bring back for us.

However, days turned into a week, and still, there was no sign of his return. We grew increasingly concerned, as our food supply dwindled, and a nationwide famine loomed ominously on the horizon. The situation grew dire, and Mum's patience wore thin. Necessity compelled us to take drastic action, for there was no food left in our abode, and we received no word from Dad for nearly a month.

In early 1994, we made the decision to return to our ancestral home in Kilungu. Our arrival was met by our ageing grandmother, who was taken aback by the sudden disappearance of her son. The uncertainty surrounding my father's fate cast a shadow over our lives, marking the beginning of a chapter fraught with challenges and uncertainty.

A New Beginning at Muthethe Primary School

In 1994, a fresh chapter of my life began as I enrolled at Muthethe Primary School, joining the ranks of Class 4. My siblings also embarked on their educational journey at the same school. News of our father's mysterious disappearance soon swept through the village, evoking pity and sympathy from our neighbours. Poverty swiftly descended upon our family, and our path in life transformed into an unexpectedly rugged terrain. My mother, faced with the daunting challenge of providing for our family, sought solace in casual labour on other people's farms.

A few months after we had settled into this new way of life, our father returned home. He bore the telltale signs of suffering, looking frail and penniless. I couldn't fathom the trials he had endured during his absence, but over time, it became apparent that he had been interdicted from his job. A conspicuous, painful wound marked his left leg, a source of curiosity and concern for all of us. Yet, he remained reticent, unwilling to divulge the circumstances that had led to his condition.

My mother became the sole provider for our family, toiling in fields, fetching water, and cutting grass to put food on our table. Meanwhile, my father remained at home, secluding himself from the world. He exhibited a short temper and a propensity for violence, frequently subjecting my mother and us to physical abuse without any apparent cause. Many sleepless nights were endured under the shadow of his unpredictable outbursts.

As the eldest son, I bore the responsibility of assisting my mother in sourcing a means of livelihood for our entire household. My heart ached for her, especially when she bore the brunt of my father's brutality. I often wondered why our father had abandoned his work, why he had become a stranger to love and had turned so violent. I attempted to broach the subject with my mother, but she remained silent in the face of my persistent inquiries, urging me instead to focus on excelling in school and taking care of my siblings.

And so, I threw myself into my studies with unwavering determination. Despite the myriad hardships and challenges that beset us, I emerged as a top performer, consistently securing the first position from Class 5 to Class 8. I harboured a resolute commitment to bringing about a change in the fortunes of my family.

A Triumph and a Search for Family

In the year 1996, a glimmer of hope graced our family as Dad was reinstated to work. He received a posting to Kyakathungu Primary School, a nearby institution, where he imparted knowledge until 1997, while I was in Class 7. However, midway through that year, he requested a transfer to Kilisa Primary School, nestled in the Nzaui Sub County near Wote town, the district headquarters. The entire family, comprising Mum and all my siblings, relocated with him. Meanwhile, I was entrusted to the care of my grandmother.

Before his transfer, Dad arranged for me to switch to St. Patrick's Kyamatheka Primary School to join Class 8, where I would sit for my KCPE (Kenya Certificate of Primary Education) in 1998. The results were nothing short of spectacular – I achieved a remarkable score of 617 marks out of a possible 700! I stood as one of the top performers in the entire Makueni district. I had shattered all previous records and set a new benchmark, one that remained unbroken by any other pupil to this very day.

However, since the family's relocation to Kilisa, there had been no communication from them whatsoever. Throughout the entire year of my Class 8, my grandmother resorted to undertaking casual farm work to procure food and paraffin for a tin lamp. Neither my grandmother nor I knew their precise whereabouts. Now, armed with my outstanding results, an intense longing to share my success with my parents engulfed me.

My cousin, Mailu, shared my determination and proposed that we embark on a journey to Kilisa in search of our family. Setting out at 3.00 am, we braved the journey on foot, charting an uncertain course to an unfamiliar destination, without a single cent for fare. We had brought along two sugarcanes as sustenance. I had no shoes; I walked barefoot, feeling every stone and pebble beneath my feet.

On our journey, we made inquiries, describing our destination as Kilisa and our father as John Maundu, a teacher. A helpful cyclist was acquainted with him and guided us towards Kilili town, where they resided. We arrived at 6 pm, having walked for over 15 gruelling hours. Our feet had swollen, our lips were parched and cracked, and we had consumed nothing but those two sugarcanes during our arduous trek.

My siblings welcomed me with elation, and my mother couldn't hold back her tears upon noticing my frail appearance and my swollen, shoeless feet. I reached into my pocket and I handed to mum my result slip. She noticed the pattern of full A’s that I had scored across all the subjects and cried with joy. She assured me that all would be well, and as we continued to talk, dad came in. He was drunk, and he was too glad to see me. His friend was pleased to see my excellent results, and dad boldly bragged in front of him that his son was a genius, just like him!

Dad came back in the dead of the night. He fueled quarrels and fights with mum, leading us to wake up; they had an argument, I don’t know why – but I was the subject of the entire disagreement. The argument got worse and we were forced to wake up again at dawn and leave with (my cousin) back to Kilungu, again walking barefoot since we literally had no money at all. We crept out while everybody was asleep, soon after the fight between my parents ended. He had already issued deadly threats and vowed that he would take me to a local day school, despite my top-grade performance…

We arrived home after another 15 hours of walking. I was tired, sad and confused as I went to sleep in my small, grass-thatched, almost falling house. The following morning, I plainly narrated to grandma all that had befallen us. I had to rest for 2 days to get back to my normal self. I then went and took the sad news to my head teacher, Mr. Kavali. He knew dad very well, and he understood the problems that faced my family…

Early January 1999, my form one admission letter from Starehe Boys’ Centre reached the head teacher. I had secured a free, fully sponsored admission for my high school. It was a great relief for me. The head teacher, together with other teachers contributed money to support me do shopping and soon, I was on the first time ever in my life boarding a bus to Nairobi accompanied by Mr. Kavali. I was admitted on 29 January, 1999. Vincent, a late cousin to dad, fully supported me with pocket money while I was at Starehe. I worked hard, fully focused on bringing light and change to my family.
